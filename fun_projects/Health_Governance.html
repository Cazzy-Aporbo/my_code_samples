Health Data Privacy, Security & AI Governance Policy (HIPAA+)

Comprehensive compliance framework exceeding HIPAA requirements

Effective Date: TBD Approved By: CEO Owners: PO, SO, AIRL

Purpose: This policy establishes a governance framework for protecting health-related data and ensuring compliant, trustworthy operation of healthcare AI products. It satisfies HIPAA requirements while incorporating controls for state privacy laws, FTC HBNR, and international regulations.

Why HIPAA+ Matters

HIPAA sets the floor for PHI protection, but modern healthcare requires more:

  • FTC HBNR: Covers consumer health apps outside HIPAA scope
  • State Laws: Additional requirements beyond federal minimums
  • AI Governance: New risks from machine learning models
  • International: GDPR special category data protections

California adds layers beyond HIPAA:

  • CPRA: "Sensitive Personal Information" rules with opt-out rights
  • CMIA: Medical information confidentiality even outside HIPAA
  • Consent: Explicit consent for certain uses
  • Rights: Deletion, correction, portability

Implementation: California AG Guidance ‚Üí

Texas imposes strict training and access timelines:

  • Training: Role-based within 90 days of hire
  • Access: Faster response times than HIPAA
  • Retention: 6-year attestation requirements
  • Controller Duties: TDPSA adds broad obligations

‚è∞ 90-Day Clock: Texas HB 300 training must be completed within 90 days of hire and after material legal changes.

Illinois has enacted groundbreaking legislation restricting AI use in behavioral health services:

  • Prohibition: AI cannot be sole provider of mental health/substance use disorder treatment
  • Human Oversight: Licensed clinician required for all diagnoses and treatment plans
  • Transparency: Mandatory disclosure when AI tools are used in care delivery
  • Consent: Explicit patient consent required for any AI involvement in therapy
  • Documentation: Must document AI tool usage in patient records

Critical Compliance: Any telehealth or digital therapeutic platform operating in Illinois must ensure human clinician oversight for all behavioral health services. AI can augment but never replace human providers.

Data Sharing & De-identification Playbook

Risk-based methods for secure healthcare data sharing

Version 1.0 Owner: Privacy Officer Last Review: Aug 2025

üéØ Objective: Enable safe data sharing for research, analytics, and AI training while protecting patient privacy through validated de-identification methods.

Data Type Risk Level Primary Method Validation Re-ID Risk

Medical Imaging High DICOM cleaning + Pixel scrubbing 100% automated scan <1% acceptable

De-identification Methods by Modality

Techniques

  • Named Entity Recognition (NER):
    • SpaCy/Stanza models trained on i2b2/n2c2 datasets
    • Custom models for institution-specific terms
    • Confidence threshold: 0.95
  • Rule-based Patterns:
    • RegEx for SSN, MRN, phone, email
    • Date shifting: ¬±30-365 days random offset
    • Geographic truncation to 3-digit ZIP
  • Context Preservation:
    • Replace with realistic surrogates [NAME_001]
    • Maintain clinical meaning and relationships
    • Consistent replacement within documents

Validation Protocol

  1. Automated metrics: Precision >0.95, Recall >0.98
  2. Manual review: 5% random sample by trained annotators
  3. Linkage testing: Attempt re-identification with public data
  4. Quarterly model retraining with error feedback

‚ö†Ô∏è High-Risk Entities: Provider names, rare diseases, and unique procedures require additional manual review.

Voice De-identification Pipeline

  1. Speaker Diarization: Identify and separate speakers
  2. Transcription: Generate text for PHI detection
  3. Voice Morphing:
    • Pitch shift: ¬±20-40% randomized
    • Formant modification: ¬±15%
    • Prosody alteration while maintaining intelligibility
  4. Segment Replacement: Silence or synthetic voice for PHI segments

Quality Metrics

  • Word Error Rate (WER): <10% post-processing
  • Speaker verification: EER >30% (unidentifiable)
  • Intelligibility: >90% word recognition

DICOM De-identification

  • Header Cleaning:
    • Remove all Type 1 & 2 identifiers per PS3.15
    • UID remapping with consistent study linkage
    • Private tag removal or evaluation
  • Pixel Data Processing:
    • OCR detection for burned-in annotations
    • Face detection and blurring for photos/3D
    • Ultrasound annotation removal
  • Validation:
    • Automated DICOM validator checking all tags
    • Visual inspection of 1% sample
    • Metadata consistency verification

Privacy-Preserving Techniques

  • k-Anonymity (k‚â•5):
    • Each record indistinguishable from k-1 others
    • Generalization hierarchies for quasi-identifiers
    • Suppression of outliers
  • l-Diversity (l‚â•3):
    • Each equivalence class has l diverse sensitive values
    • Prevents attribute disclosure
  • Differential Privacy (Œµ=1.0):
    • Calibrated noise addition
    • Formal privacy guarantees
    • Query-based or data release

Utility Preservation

Balance privacy vs utility through:

  • Information loss metrics (ILM)
  • Statistical property preservation
  • Use-case specific optimization

Model Card Requirements

  • Model Name & Version: Unique identifier and semantic versioning
  • Purpose: Intended use cases and clinical context
  • Architecture: Algorithm type, parameters, complexity
  • Training Data: Sources, size, date ranges, inclusion/exclusion
  • Dependencies: Required libraries, APIs, infrastructure
  • Primary Metrics: AUROC, sensitivity, specificity, F1
  • Calibration: Brier score, calibration plots
  • Confidence Intervals: Bootstrap or cross-validation CIs
  • Subgroup Analysis: Performance by demographics, conditions
  • Comparison: Baseline, clinical standard, prior version
  • Protected Attributes: Age, gender, race, ethnicity, SES
  • Fairness Metrics:
    • Demographic parity
    • Equalized odds
    • Calibration by group
  • Bias Sources: Historical, representation, measurement, aggregation
  • Mitigation: Pre-processing, in-processing, post-processing methods
  • Known Limitations: Edge cases, failure modes, constraints
  • Safety Mechanisms: Confidence thresholds, human review triggers
  • Monitoring Plan: KPIs, alert thresholds, review cadence
  • Rollback Criteria: Performance degradation triggers
  • Incident Response: Escalation path, communication plan

Illinois AI in Mental Health Compliance Checklist

Critical Alert: Non-compliance with Illinois AI mental health regulations can result in professional license revocation and criminal charges.

Mandatory Requirements

  • Licensed Clinician Oversight
  • ‚òê Licensed mental health professional assigned to all AI-assisted cases
  • ‚òê Clinician reviews all AI-generated recommendations before implementation
  • ‚òê Direct clinician-patient relationship established and maintained
  • Patient Disclosure & Consent
  • ‚òê Written disclosure provided about AI use in treatment
  • ‚òê Explicit written consent obtained before AI deployment
  • ‚òê Option to opt-out of AI-assisted care provided
  • ‚òê Alternative treatment options explained
  • Documentation Requirements
  • ‚òê AI tool usage documented in patient records
  • ‚òê Clinician review of AI outputs documented
  • ‚òê Patient consent forms retained
  • ‚òê Decision audit trail maintained
  • Prohibited Activities
  • ‚òê No autonomous AI diagnosis
  • ‚òê No AI-only treatment sessions
  • ‚òê No AI prescription generation without clinician review
  • ‚òê No AI crisis intervention without human backup

Implementation Steps

  1. System Architecture Review - Ensure human oversight capabilities
  2. Workflow Modification - Insert clinician checkpoints
  3. Consent Form Updates - Include AI disclosure language
  4. Staff Training - Educate on compliance requirements
  5. Audit Procedures - Regular compliance verification
  6. Incident Response Plan - Prepare for regulatory inquiries

Sample Disclosure Language

"This practice utilizes artificial intelligence tools to assist in treatment planning and clinical decision-making. However, all diagnoses, treatment recommendations, and therapeutic interventions are reviewed and approved by a licensed mental health professional. You have the right to receive treatment without AI assistance if you prefer."
